Session 9 · Foundation

The Coaching
Mindset Shift

S9 · Core

The Fundamental Shift: Traditional leaders are trained to be the expert — to solve, decide, and direct. Coaching leaders develop a radically different instinct: when someone brings them a problem, they ask rather than tell. This is not inefficiency — it is the most powerful multiplier of organizational capability available to a senior leader.

⚡
Traditional Leadership Mode — Solving
"Here's what I think you should do..." — provides the answer
Focused on the problem — drives for quick resolution
Creates dependency — team brings problems, leader solves them
Builds leader's authority — diminishes team's initiative
Scales linearly with the leader's time and bandwidth
Tells: "The answer is X. Make sure you do Y by Friday."
🌱
Coaching Leadership Mode — Enabling
"What options have you already considered?" — unlocks thinking
Focused on the person — develops capability for next time
Creates autonomy — team develops the capacity to solve their own problems
Builds team's authority — amplifies organizational intelligence
Scales exponentially — the leader's impact multiplies through others
Asks: "What would success look like? What would you do if you knew?"

The Art of
Powerful Questions

The 3 Levels of Listening — Co-Active Coaching / Whitworth
1
Internal Listening
Listening through your own filter — planning what to say next, relating to your own experience, judging. This is where most people naturally listen.
⚠ "I'm thinking about what to say when they finish..."
2
Focused Listening
Fully focused on the other person — their words, meaning, and emotional state. You notice what they say and how they say it. Coaches operate here as a minimum.
✓ "I'm fully present with what they are telling me."
3
Global Listening ★
Listening to everything — words, tone, pace, body language, silence, energy, and what is NOT being said. The master coaching level. Picks up the subtle signals that reveal the real issue.
★ "I notice you said 'we should' but your energy dropped when you said it."
Question Taxonomy — From Surface to Transformational
🔍
Clarifying Questions
Surface the specifics. Transform vague statements into precise, workable realities.
"When you say 'the team isn't engaged' — what specifically are you observing?"
🔭
Perspective Questions
Shift the view. Unlock insight by changing the angle of observation.
"If your best team member were watching this situation — what would they notice?"
⚡
Challenge Questions
Disrupt assumptions. Surface limiting beliefs that are blocking progress.
"What would you do if you knew you couldn't fail?"
🌟
Generative Questions
Create new possibility. Move from problem-focus to solution-creation.
"Imagine this is fully resolved — what does that future look like for you?"

Session 9 · Star Framework

The GROW
Coaching Framework

GROW · Core

Origin: Developed by Sir John Whitmore in the 1980s — validated over 40 years of executive coaching practice. GROW is the world's most widely used coaching conversation structure, adopted by Google, Microsoft, Unilever, and thousands of organisations globally. It works because it mirrors the natural structure of effective human problem-solving.

G
Goal
What do you want?
Establish a clear, motivating goal for the conversation and the longer-term. Distinguish between end goals (what you ultimately want) and performance goals (what you can control).
"What would you like to achieve in this conversation?"
"What does success look like — specifically?"
"How will you know when you've reached it?"
"What matters most to you about this goal?"
R
Reality
Where are you now?
Explore the current situation honestly and objectively. Help the coachee see clearly — without judgment. Surface both what is working and what is not. Resist the urge to jump to solutions.
"What is happening right now — describe it as it actually is?"
"What have you already tried?"
"What is working that you could build on?"
"What impact is this situation having on you?"
O
Options
What could you do?
Generate a wide range of possible actions. Quantity first, quality second. The coach's job is to expand the coachee's thinking — not to provide the answer. Do not evaluate options prematurely.
"What are all the possible options — even the ones that seem unrealistic?"
"What would you do if you had unlimited resources?"
"What's been done in similar situations that you could adapt?"
"What else? And what else beyond that?"
W
Will / Way Forward
What will you do?
Convert insight and options into a specific, committed action plan. Who does what, by when? Secure genuine commitment — not compliance. Test the commitment level and address barriers.
"Which option will you commit to — and by when?"
"On a scale of 1–10, how committed are you to this?"
"What might stop you — and how will you handle it?"
"What support do you need from me?"

Session 9 & 10 · Advanced Tools

Feedback &
Delegation Intelligence

S9 · S10

Why these two capabilities define Coaching Leadership: Feedback is the mechanism by which learning is accelerated — it is how coaches help people see what they cannot yet see themselves. Delegation is how coaches extend their impact — distributing authority to build capability rather than retaining control to remain indispensable.

The SBI Feedback Model — Center for Creative Leadership
S
📍
Situation
When & Where
Describe the specific situation — time, place, context. Anchors the feedback in observable reality rather than generalisation. Avoids the "you always" trap.
"In the project review meeting on Tuesday morning, with the full leadership team present..."
B
👁️
Behavior
What I Observed
Describe the specific, observable behavior — what was said or done. No interpretations, judgments, or assumptions about intent. Behavior is what you can see and hear.
"...when the junior team member raised their concern, you interrupted them twice and redirected to another speaker..."
I
💡
Impact
The Effect It Had
Describe the impact on you, the team, the project, or the culture. Personal and specific. This is where the coaching conversation begins — what did they intend vs. what actually happened?
"...I noticed the team member went quiet for the rest of the meeting, and two others looked uncomfortable. The idea wasn't explored."
Situational Leadership II — Delegation by Development Level (Blanchard)
D1 — Enthusiastic Beginner
Low competence · High commitment
New to the task. Motivated but lacks skills. Eager to learn.
Leader Style
Direct — S1
Provide clear instruction, structure, and close follow-up. Tell what, when, and how.
D2 — Disillusioned Learner
Some competence · Low commitment
Developing but frustrated. Realises the task is harder than expected.
Leader Style
Coach — S2
Explain and clarify. Listen and affirm. Use GROW conversations to rebuild confidence.
D3 — Capable but Cautious
High competence · Variable commitment
Has the skill but lacks confidence or motivation. Needs reassurance.
Leader Style
Support — S3
Listen, collaborate, encourage. Ask rather than tell. Facilitate problem-solving without taking over.
D4 — Self-Reliant Achiever
High competence · High commitment
Skilled and motivated. Ready for full autonomy and ownership.
Leader Style
Delegate — S4
Step back. Set the outcome and trust the process. Check in without checking up. Enable full ownership.
💬

"For each person on your team: which development level (D1–D4) are they at for their key responsibilities — and are you leading them at the right style, or are you over-directing your D4s and under-supporting your D2s?"

My Coaching Practice

Your Personal
Coaching Protocol

This 5-step protocol is designed to be used in two ways: as a self-coaching practice you apply to your own leadership challenges, and as a team coaching conversation structure you apply in your 1:1s, team meetings, and development conversations.

1
Pause & Presence
Create the Coaching Space
Before any coaching conversation — with yourself or others — create a deliberate pause. Put away distractions. Set a clear intention: "This next 20 minutes is about development, not tasks." The quality of your presence determines the quality of the conversation.
Mindful PauseLevel-3 ListeningClear Intent
2
Surface the Real Issue
Ask Before You Assume
The first topic raised is rarely the real topic. Start with: "What do you most want to focus on — and what's really going on beneath that?" For self-coaching: "What is actually bothering me about this — and is that the real issue?"
Clarifying QuestionsIceberg ModelActive Listening
3
GROW the Conversation
Structure for Insight
Apply the GROW framework deliberately. Resist jumping to Options before spending real time on Goal (clarity of what success means) and Reality (honest current state). This is the most commonly skipped step — and the most important one.
GROW ModelPowerful QuestionsChallenge Assumptions
4
Secure Real Commitment
From Insight to Action
Coaching without commitment is conversation. Always end with a specific action, a specific timeline, and a specific accountability structure. "What will you do, by when — and how will I know?" Test commitment on a 1-10 scale. Anything below 8 needs a different plan.
GROW Will PhaseCommitment ScaleAction Planning
5
Close & Reflect
The Coaching Loop
Close every coaching conversation by reflecting on the conversation itself: "What was most valuable about this for you?" and "What did I notice as a coach?" Schedule a follow-up — coaching without follow-up loses 60% of its impact. The loop is what makes it developmental.
ReflectionFollow-Up ProtocolSBI Feedback
Proposals for Your Team — Apply This Week

Replace one task-focused 1:1 per week with a pure coaching conversation. The agenda: one question — "What is the most important thing you want to think through this week, professionally?" Use Steps 2–5 of the protocol. No solving. No advice. Pure GROW. Document what they commit to and review it next week. Do this for 4 weeks and observe the change in initiative, confidence, and self-direction.

Once a month, gather your team for a 60-minute peer coaching session. One person brings a real leadership challenge. The rest of the team — not you — coaches them through GROW. Your role: facilitate and observe. Debrief together: what questions were most powerful? This develops coaching capability across the whole team simultaneously and creates a culture of developmental dialogue.

Every other Friday, each team member sends you one SBI-structured observation — about anything: a meeting, a decision, a behaviour they've noticed in the team or in themselves. You respond to each one with one coaching question. This creates a written coaching dialogue, normalises feedback, and builds psychological safety around honest observation over time.

Four times a year, hold a structured 60-minute development conversation with each direct report. Agenda: (1) Where are you now — what are you proudest of and what has challenged you most? (2) Where do you want to go — vision, aspiration, next level? (3) What is the gap — what would you need to develop? (4) What is the plan — one specific stretch goal for the next quarter? This is the 20% of the 70-20-10 model — the most powerful lever you have for talent development.

Every Friday, spend 10 minutes coaching yourself through four questions: (1) What am I most proud of as a leader this week? (2) What one situation did I handle less well than I wanted to — what would I do differently? (3) Who did I develop this week — and how? (4) What is my one coaching commitment for next week? Write the answers. Review them monthly. This is the most underused leadership development tool available — and it costs only 10 minutes.

"Coaching is a practice, not an event. The leaders who transform organizations are the ones who coach in the corridor, in the corridor, at the whiteboard, and in the five minutes before a meeting starts — not just in formal sessions."

Google's Project Oxygen — What Makes a Great Manager?
Google · Technology · Global · 2008–Present

Google set out to prove that managers don't matter — and instead proved the opposite. After analyzing 10,000 manager performance reviews, employee surveys, and nominations for top-manager awards, they identified the 8 behaviors of Google's best managers. Coaching was number one.

"Good managers coach. Bad managers tell. And the difference in team outcomes between these two approaches is not marginal — it is transformational."

Google Project Oxygen Finding
The 8 Behaviors — Behavior #1: Be a Good Coach

Google's research found that the single most important behavior of their best managers was being an effective coach. This meant: giving specific feedback, having regular one-on-ones focused on development (not tasks), and empowering team members to solve their own problems rather than solving them. Managers who scored highest on coaching had teams that were significantly more productive, retained their best people longer, and reported higher satisfaction.

What They Changed

Google trained every manager in coaching skills — specifically active listening, powerful questioning, and structured feedback. They redesigned 1:1 meeting templates to include coaching elements. They measured coaching quality as part of manager performance reviews — making it a professional accountability, not just a soft skill workshop.

IBM — Embedding the Leader-as-Coach Model Globally
IBM · Technology / Consulting · Global · 2015–Present

With 350,000+ employees across 170 countries, IBM needed a scalable model for leadership development that didn't require external coaching for every person. Their solution: train every senior leader to be a coach — creating an internal coaching capability that scaled without additional cost.

"We discovered that the highest-impact leadership development wasn't what happened in the classroom. It was what happened in the conversation between a manager and their team member the Monday morning after."

IBM HR Learning Research
The Leader-as-Coach Programme

IBM trained over 3,000 senior leaders globally in coaching skills using a model that directly parallels what you are learning today: structured coaching conversations, powerful questioning, and GROW-based frameworks. Leaders were certified in coaching capability — not as an optional extra but as a core leadership competency evaluated in their annual reviews.

The Multiplier Effect

Each trained leader was expected to coach at least 3 team members — who were then equipped to coach their own teams. This created a coaching cascade across the organization: 3,000 trained leaders × 3 people each = 9,000 people receiving regular coaching, at near-zero marginal cost. The 70-20-10 model in institutional action.

Microsoft — The Growth Mindset as Coaching Foundation
Microsoft · Technology · Global · 2014–Present

When Satya Nadella became CEO in 2014, he made Carol Dweck's Growth Mindset the philosophical foundation of Microsoft's entire leadership culture. The premise: if leaders coach through a growth mindset — believing capability is developable rather than fixed — the entire organisation transforms.

"Our culture shift from 'know-it-all' to 'learn-it-all' was ultimately a coaching shift. Leaders stopped proving their expertise and started developing others'."

Satya Nadella, CEO Microsoft
The Coaching Culture Architecture

Microsoft redesigned its entire performance management system around coaching principles. They eliminated the stack-ranking system (which destroyed collaboration and coaching) and replaced it with a model that explicitly measured how well leaders developed their team members. A manager's review now included: "How much did you grow the people around you this year?"

The Systemic Result

Within three years of embedding coaching-culture principles, Microsoft's market capitalization more than doubled — from $300B to over $700B. Employee engagement scores reached decade highs. Cross-team collaboration increased dramatically. The coaching shift was not just a talent play — it was the primary driver of Microsoft's return to innovation leadership.

Unilever — Coaching at Scale for 148,000 People
Unilever · FMCG · Global · 2010–Present

Unilever built one of the world's most recognized leadership development programmes — the Unilever Leadership Development Programme — with coaching at its core. They had a clear belief: the competitive advantage of a 148,000-person organisation is the quality of its conversations between leaders and their teams.

"We don't just send people on leadership programmes. We build coaching into every conversation, every review, every project debrief. Leadership development happens in the moments between the formal events."

Unilever HR Strategy
The Coaching Architecture

Unilever trained all senior leaders in coaching skills using a model that combined GROW-based conversation frameworks with structured feedback tools. They created "coaching moments" — building brief coaching check-ins into team meetings, project reviews, and even town halls. The principle: coaching is not a separate activity — it is how leadership happens.

The Talent Multiplier

Unilever's internal coaching culture became their primary talent retention mechanism. Their top talent stayed not because of compensation alone — but because they felt genuinely developed. In a 2019 global survey, 89% of Unilever employees said they had regular developmental conversations with their manager. The coaching culture directly drove their ability to develop leaders from within rather than recruiting externally.

The 90-Day
Coaching Architecture

01
Days 1–30 · Awareness
Build the Foundation
  • Complete your coaching diagnostic and share it with one trusted direct report for their perspective.
  • Introduce GROW into your next three 1:1 meetings — tell the person what you're doing and why.
  • Start the 10-minute Friday self-coaching reflection. Write answers to the four questions.
  • Identify one person on your team who would most benefit from a dedicated coaching conversation this month.
  • Practice Level-3 Listening in your next three team meetings — notice what you were missing before.
02
Days 31–60 · Practice
Build the Habits
  • Hold a pure coaching 1:1 with each direct report — use the 5-step protocol. No solving allowed.
  • Deliver three SBI feedback conversations — one developmental, one appreciative, one corrective.
  • Run one Team Coaching Circle: bring your team together, one person brings a real challenge, the group coaches using GROW.
  • Practice five questions from the powerful questions bank in real conversations — note which generate the most insight.
  • Map each direct report to a Blanchard development level (D1–D4) — adjust your leadership style accordingly.
03
Days 61–90 · Integration
Build the Culture
  • Hold quarterly development conversations with each direct report — structured, 60-minute, GROW-based.
  • Launch the Feedback Friday Habit with your team — make peer feedback a bi-weekly norm.
  • Ask your team: "What is one thing I could do differently as your coach that would help you most?" Listen without defending.
  • Revisit your diagnostic — compare to Day 1. What has shifted? What still needs intentional focus?
  • Identify one person you will formally mentor in coaching skills — pass it on.

Module C — System Integration

From Coaching Skills
to Coaching Culture

The four capabilities of Module C — coaching mindset, powerful questions, GROW mastery, and high-quality feedback — are a complete system. Each reinforces the others when practised consistently and intentionally.

🔄

Coaching unlocks Belonging (Module B)

A leader who coaches creates psychological safety — the foundation of belonging. When people feel developed rather than judged, they trust more, collaborate better, and bring their full thinking to the team. Coaching is Lencioni's trust foundation in practice.

🌱

Coaching enables Delegation (Module D)

You cannot delegate effectively to someone who isn't developed. Coaching builds the capability, confidence, and judgment that make delegation safe — for both leader and team member. Module C is the prerequisite for Module D.

❓

Questions amplify Adaptability (Module A)

The OODA Loop and Ladder of Inference from Module A require exactly the kind of thinking that powerful coaching questions develop. A team coached to question assumptions and reframe situations is exponentially more adaptive than one that is told what to think.

⭐

The compounding effect

A coaching leader who has mastered Modules A, B, and C creates an exponential effect: they lead teams that are adaptive (A), collaborative (B), and self-developing (C) — teams that improve themselves without constant intervention. This is the architecture of organizational capability that outlasts any individual leader.
